Week 9

  • Tue 29 May
    • Discussion of topic for final paper (2500 words):

      We live in a period of great freedom (can we call it neo-liberalism?). Features of it are that we are autonomous (protective of our autonomy and completely opposed to any form of heteronomy), yet connected through global markets whose laws we accept and use. We insist on the defense of everyone's access to full human rights, while being concerned about fitting, day to day, in a complex, interconnected world.

      What meaning can a study of Genesis and its history have for a modern person? Is it just a historical curiosity, even though it may still have some importance because of its long influence on the three monotheistic traditions, on art and literature? Has it run its course socially (because of the globalism of our world) and ethically (kinship structures and conceptions of gender have changed). Are we done with its assumed heteronomy (revealed law)?

      Or is it still important to study this text because, once the historical situation of its writers is better understood and taken seriously, it still speaks to some aspects of our experience? Parts of the text that would help in discussing this question are the stories of creation, the story cycle of Abraham and Sarah, and the Jacob saga, with its trickery and more complex relationships.

Week 10

  • Tue 5 June
    • Last class:
    1. Discussion of the last paper and the themes that can by pursued in answering the basic question: “Why is it still important to study Genesis and its historical context?” A number of themes were proposed in the general discussion. Here is a suggestive list that I collected after the class. Some may be merged as they recouped each other:
      • An analysis of how modern fiction used Genesis in its story-telling and exploration of values, ancient an modern. A book by Dara Horn,, A guide for the perplexed, was suggested in class (Nomi_) A film like East of Eden could be used also.
      • Does Genesis introduce a human history that is open to the future and presents hope as a value? How does it do that? This may take the form of an analysis of the story of creation, the notion of the humans as images of the divine, the nature of the choices offered to the first humans, Cain and Abel, Abraham and Sarah, Jacob and his family, and Joseph. How different is this openness from the kind of ideology and cosmologies that were used by ancient kingdoms to justify the return to order and to a putative Golden Age? Why was order and absence of chaos so important for ancient political regimes? The question could be extended to modern regimes and their occasional attempt to return to a golden age or some form of equilibrium. Finally, when and why did the authors of Genesis come up with this view of the world and the role of humankind in it?
      • Another theme that we haven't explored in depth in the class but is important is the nature and origin of sin. We don't use that word in modern times, though we tend to remain very demanding and sometimes censorious in our ethical views of the world. The word “sin” appears for the first time in the story of Cain and Abel (Gen 4). The concept of sin, be it error or a more complex mix of mistake and moral intention ("evil impulse" or rebellion), was common to all ancient cultures that were mentioned in class. Do you think that polytheistic societies had a solid explanation for sin, as provided by their cosmologies? And doesn't the notion of a perfect world, as described in Genesis 1:1--2:4a, make an explanation for the existence of sin difficult if not impossible?
      • The question of the usefulness of reading Genesis in modern times could start with an examination of the notions of freedom and slavery as they crop up in Genesis. There is no specific word being used for “freedom” in Genesis but the idea of liberation, or rather salvation by God, is clearly fundamental. How do the choices of Adam and Eve, Abraham and Sarah (vs Lot), Rebekah, Jacob vs Esau, and especially Joseph, illustrate their search for independence and freedom? Are similar issues present in modern society? That is, are present people, so keen on their freedoms, still struggling with slavery even though slavery proper has been nearly eradicated?
      • One aspect of Genesis that we have seen time and again in class is the conflict or tension between first born son(s) and the younger son(s). How is it developed? It is a straighforward business to follow this theme through the stories of Cain and Abel, Ishmael and Isaac, Esau and Jacob, Leah and Rachel, the ten brothers and Joseph (plus Benjamin, who seems left out).
        But it is not as simple to come up with reasons why the authors of Genesis framed this conflict as they did. Was there a political and theological goal being pursued? Was their promotion of younger sons (and even daughters in the case of Rachel) a way to introduce a vision of history that was at odds with what was normally practiced?
        Finally, in modern society, is there something comparable going on in our vision of politics? I'm thinking of the way we can be very critical of power and protective of the weak, suspicious about force and looking for grace.
      • Last theme I think about---and again, the suggestions and questions I'm collecting here can be mixed and reshaped, of course, in forming your own answer to the main question (Why read Genesis now?)---the last theme is the question of ethnic development or what I called ethnogenesis. What do the stories of Genesis tell the reader about the notion of a people, of a nation? Is it defined mainly by genetics (biologically, or by biological descent), or is it shaped by choice? The critical texts here are: the story of Abraham, from Genesis 12 to 25; the story of Lot and daughters; and finally the story of Joseph who becomes an Egyptian, leading even to a change of name. We see Judah in Genesis 38 become nearly Canaanite. The brothers of Joseph, during their visits to Egypt, say repeatedly that they come from Canaan. Yet, Canaanites are reviled by the rest of the Bible. The full answer requires consideration of the book of Exodus in which the people of Israel are defined by accepting the covenant they receive directly at Sinai, before having a land (the “promised land”). But it is possible to answer this question without knowledge of Exodus by reflecting on the stories I mentioned above.
        It would be good to think at the same time about the modern situation and what constitutes us as people. Does Genesis help with rethinking and redefining our modern idea of belonging?
    2. Overview of Genesis 42--45: the story of the double trip to Egypt by the brothers, the recognition scene, and the decision by Jacob to go to Egypt to see his favorite son, Joseph. In class, I went over five themes that are of varying importance when trying to explain the goals of the author(s) of this novella that goes from Gen 37 to Gen 50:
      • The story of Joseph supplies an explanation for the presence of all descendants of Jacob in Egypt. After many years, the “Israelites” (descendants of Jacob) are all participants in the story of Exodus, which is characterized by four components:
        • a story of salvation or liberation;
        • the story of the giving of the law or of the covenant, done directly by God;
        • the promise of the land (conditional on accepting the covenant);
        • the desert sanctuary as guide (with a mobile divinity);
      • The brothers' hate for Joseph and the famine in Canaan turn out to be providential. The divine will or plan is now hidden behind complex appearances. It actually uses downturns (prison and slavery) into liberation and victories. In the stories of creation, Abraham, and Jacob, the divine voice came from outside, in the fullness of day (though occasionally in dreams for Jacob). Now it is built in, still as an external, single divine will, but inherent (immanent) to the historical process.
      • The development of providence in the story means that interpretation of dreams and other voices or signs is now required to figure out what divine will is (or in modern terms: the historical process). The knowledge of things to come is not accessible to “magicians” or “wise men” of Egypt. There is some ethnical pride attached to the “knowledgeable” Joseph (as for Daniel in the book of Daniel, in a similar situation), as compared to the failing wisdom of Egypt (famous in antiquity).
      • There is a royal undercurrent or theme: the story of Joseph justifies the existence of the kingdoms of Israel and Judah, while incorporating criticisms and failures right from the beginning. Reuben, Judah, Simeon and Levi are blamed for their failure. They are the ancestors of the northern and southern Israelite kings. Further, both Joseph and Judah are presented as losing their ethnic identity or endangering it. Judah becomes almost a Canaanite, and Joseph an Egyptian. This is of course from the point of view of a narrator who is reflecting on the political and theological situation long after the fall of the real kingdoms of Israel and Judah.
      • Lastly, there is the theme of the solidarity of brothers (Gen 42--45), in spite of the initial jealousy and hate. This solidarity and renewed kinship may be a reflection of the important theme of brotherhood developed in the books of Deuteronomy and Ezra-Nehemiah.
